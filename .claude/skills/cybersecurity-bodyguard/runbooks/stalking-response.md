# Stalking Response

Use when a specific individual is surveilling, following, or showing up near Alex or Alex's partner/family. Physical safety is the primary concern; all other protocols are secondary.

## Principle: believe the pattern

Stalking is recognized by *pattern*, not single incidents. If you find yourself rationalizing ("it's probably a coincidence," "they're probably just being nice"), that's signal. Log everything. Let someone else decide if it's "enough."

## Immediate (right now)

1. **If you are in immediate danger, call 911.** Not 988, not a hotline — 911. Tell them "I am being stalked and feel unsafe right now."
2. **Move to a public, well-lit, populated place.** Coffee shop, grocery store, hotel lobby. Do not go home if you think you're being followed there.
3. **Tell one trusted person in real time.** Share location via iMessage or Google Maps. Someone should know where you are until this is resolved.
4. **Do not confront.** Do not "just ask them what they want." Stalkers interpret contact as engagement and engagement as encouragement.

## Within 24 hours

### File a police report

Even if the incident feels minor. The report establishes a record that is essential for:
- Obtaining a restraining order / order of protection
- Prosecuting if it escalates
- Proving a pattern to skeptical platforms and employers

Steps:
1. Call the non-emergency line of your local PD. Ask for the detective on duty or the cyberstalking / domestic violence unit.
2. Bring: a written timeline of all incidents, all evidence (screenshots, texts, photos, witness names), a description of the stalker (name if known, physical description, vehicle, license plate).
3. Get the case number and the detective's name + direct line. Write them down.
4. If the stalker is identifiable and there's evidence of threats, ask specifically about an Emergency Protective Order (EPO) or equivalent — many states allow one within 24 hours.

### Notify your circle

Tell, at minimum:
- Your partner (who also needs to harden — stalkers pivot through intimate partners)
- A trusted family member
- One trusted friend nearby
- Your employer's HR or security if stalker knows your workplace
- Your building manager / doorman if applicable
- Neighbors you trust (ask them to report suspicious vehicles or approaches)

Share a photo of the stalker if you have one. Tell each of them: "If this person contacts you about me, do not engage, record everything, and tell me immediately."

### Physical hardening

- [ ] Vary your daily routes and times. Stalkers rely on predictable patterns.
- [ ] Do not post real-time location on social media. Delay posts by hours/days minimum.
- [ ] Disable location services for social apps (Twitter/X, Instagram, Facebook, Strava, Snapchat, Bumble/Hinge/etc. if applicable).
- [ ] Close Strava / fitness app public profiles — Strava segments have been used to locate people.
- [ ] Check your car for AirTags and other trackers. Walk around with your iPhone — iOS will alert you to unknown AirTags traveling with you. Android users can download "Tracker Detect" from Apple.
- [ ] Check for unusual gifts, items mailed to you, or items left near your door. Do not touch — photograph, bag, and deliver to PD.
- [ ] If a stalker has been inside your home (ex-partner, etc.), change locks and check for hidden cameras. Professional sweeps are ~$200-500 and worth it.
- [ ] Consider temporary relocation if pattern escalates. An Airbnb or hotel for a few days costs less than you'd think; safety is worth it.

### Digital hardening

- [ ] Rotate all passwords. Use a password manager (1Password, Bitwarden).
- [ ] Enable 2FA everywhere. Use an authenticator app (Authy, 1Password) not SMS — SIM swaps are a common stalker escalation.
- [ ] Call your phone carrier and add a port-out PIN. T-Mobile, Verizon, AT&T all offer this. Prevents SIM swaps.
- [ ] Check all email accounts for forwarding rules and OAuth app authorizations — stalkers with prior access sometimes leave persistence.
- [ ] Check iCloud Family Sharing, Google Family, Find My, and any shared calendars / photo libraries for surveillance.
- [ ] Check your home Wi-Fi for unknown devices. Change the Wi-Fi password.
- [ ] If stalker had prior physical access to your devices, consider factory reset of phone and laptop. Stalkerware is a real threat.
- [ ] Review https://stopstalkerware.org/ for a full protocol if an ex-partner or household member is the stalker — that scenario has unique risks.

## Restraining orders / orders of protection

Requirements vary by state. Generally you need:
- Evidence of a pattern (2+ incidents)
- Specific acts or threats
- A police report

Resources:
- WomensLaw.org: https://www.womenslaw.org/laws — state-by-state restraining order guides (applies to all genders despite the name)
- Victim Connect: 1-855-4-VICTIM (1-855-484-2846) — will help you find local advocates and walk through the process
- Local DV shelters have advocates who help file, even for non-DV stalking

## Resources

- **National Stalking Hotline (Stalking Prevention, Awareness, and Resource Center)**: 1-855-4-VICTIM
- **NNEDV Safety Net**: https://www.techsafety.org/ — digital safety for survivors
- **EFF surveillance self-defense**: https://ssd.eff.org/
- **Crisis Text Line**: text HOME to 741741
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline**: if the stress becomes unbearable

## What not to do

- Do not "confront and set boundaries." Stalkers interpret confrontation as progress.
- Do not publicly call out the stalker on social media. It escalates and can destroy your legal case.
- Do not accept an "apology" or request to "just talk." These are access attempts.
- Do not underestimate. 76% of intimate partner femicide victims were stalked first (J. McFarlane et al., 1999, the foundational study in this area). Take it seriously from day one.

## Long term

Stalking cases can last years. Sustainable response requires:

- A regular check-in with a therapist who specializes in trauma / stalking cases
- A single point of contact at your PD (the detective who owns your case)
- A lawyer for civil remedies if criminal proceedings are slow
- A support person who knows the full situation — do not carry it alone

This runbook is a starting point, not a substitute for professional help. Call VictimConnect (1-855-4-VICTIM) — a trained advocate will walk through your specific situation.
